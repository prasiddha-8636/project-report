\chapter{INTRODUCTION}

\section{Background}
Satellite navigation signals weaken or vanish in deep Himalayan valleys due to topographic shielding and multi-path interference \cite{pan2020camera, Steger2022}. Canyon walls, steep ridges, and dense cloud cover block the line of sight to GNSS satellites, leaving trekkers and rescue teams without reliable coordinates. The mountain skyline, however, is always visible from any open viewpoint and its shape is tied directly to the observer's position \cite{pan2020camera}. If a system can extract that shape from a photograph and match it against a known database of terrain profiles, it can determine where the photo was taken---without any satellite signal \cite{Steger2022, pan2020camera}. Researchers proved this approach in European mountain ranges \cite{baatz2012large, saurer2016image} and hilly terrain in China \cite{pan2020camera}.

\section{Motivation}
Navigation failure in the Khumbu region can be fatal. Trekkers routinely carry smartphones with cameras, and these cameras can serve as positioning sensors if the right software is available. An offline skyline-matching system would give users a backup that works even in GNSS-denied areas, independent of cell towers or internet connectivity.

\section{Problem Statement}
The goal is to estimate a camera's geographic coordinates and viewing direction from a single mountain photograph. Three practical challenges make this difficult:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Extraction:} Haze, clouds, and low contrast frequently obscure the skyline boundary \cite{Yang2025}.
\item \textbf{Search scale:} Without knowing which direction the camera is pointing, the system must search across all possible orientations for every candidate location in the database \cite{pan2020camera}.
\item \textbf{Data scarcity:} Annotated mountain photographs are rare for the Himalayas, so synthetic training images must supplement real data \cite{Brejcha2017}.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Objectives}
\begin{itemize}
\item Generate a 360-degree horizon database from the Copernicus GLO-30 Digital Elevation Model.
\item Build a matching pipeline that combines Euclidean distance search with Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) to resolve coordinates from photographs.
\end{itemize}
